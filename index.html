Pre-Investment Checklist

Before committing any capital, work through these essential questions. Pair them with KeepRule Principles for a complete framework.

1. Business Understanding

Can you explain this business to a child?
  • Can you describe what the company does in one simple sentence?
  • Do you understand how the company makes money?
  • Can you identify the company's primary customers?
  • Do you understand the industry dynamics and competitive landscape?
  • Is this business within your circle of competence?
  • Could you run this business if you had to?

Green Flag

You can explain the business model, competitive advantages, and key risks to someone with no financial background in under two minutes.

2. Competitive Advantage (Moat)

What protects this business from competition?
  • Does the company have a durable competitive advantage?
  • Is the moat getting wider or narrower over time?
  • Can you identify the specific source: brand, network effects, switching costs, cost advantages, or patents?
  • How would a well-funded competitor attack this business?
  • Has the company maintained pricing power over time?
  • Are profit margins stable or improving over 5-10 years?

3. Management Quality

Would you trust these people with your money?
  • Does management have a track record of rational capital allocation?
  • Do insiders own significant shares (aligned incentives)?
  • Is management compensation reasonable relative to performance?
  • Does the company have a history of honest communication with shareholders?
  • Has management delivered on past promises and guidance?
  • Is the CEO focused on long-term value creation, not short-term stock price?

"In evaluating people, you look for three qualities: integrity, intelligence, and energy. If they do not have the first, the other two will kill you."

-- Warren Buffett (read more at KeepRule Masters)

4. Financial Health

Can this business survive the worst-case scenario?
  • Is the balance sheet strong (low or manageable debt)?
  • Does the company generate consistent free cash flow?
  • Is return on equity (ROE) consistently above 15%?
  • Are revenue and earnings growing steadily?
  • Can the company fund growth internally without diluting shareholders?
  • Could the business survive a severe recession?
  • Is working capital management efficient?

5. Valuation

Is the price right?
  • What is the intrinsic value based on conservative assumptions?
  • Is the current price below intrinsic value with a margin of safety?
  • What are you paying relative to earnings, free cash flow, and book value?
  • How does the valuation compare to historical averages?
  • What growth rate is the market implying at the current price?
  • Would you be comfortable buying the entire business at this price?

Red Flag

If the stock requires aggressive growth assumptions to justify its current price, you are speculating, not investing. Always demand a margin of safety. Explore this principle at KeepRule Principles.

Red Flags and Warning Signs

Any single red flag should trigger deeper investigation. Multiple red flags are a strong signal to walk away. Learn to identify these patterns with KeepRule Blog.

Financial Red Flags

Aggressive Revenue Recognition

Revenue growing much faster than cash flow from operations. This often indicates management is booking revenue that has not been truly earned yet.

Rising Debt Relative to Earnings

Debt growing faster than operating income year over year. The company may be borrowing to mask declining business fundamentals.

Frequent "One-Time" Charges

Recurring restructuring or special charges that management asks you to ignore. If they happen every year, they are not one-time.

Inventory Building Faster Than Sales

Growing inventory relative to revenue suggests products are not selling, and future write-downs may be coming.

Declining Cash Conversion

Earnings growing but free cash flow flat or declining. Earnings quality is deteriorating -- cash does not lie.

Management Red Flags

Excessive Insider Selling

Multiple executives selling large portions of their holdings. They know the business best -- their actions speak louder than their words.

Constantly Moving Goalposts

Management frequently misses guidance then changes metrics or definitions of success. This is a sign of either incompetence or dishonesty.

Complex Corporate Structures

Unnecessarily complex entities, off-balance-sheet vehicles, or related-party transactions. Complexity often hides problems.

Blaming External Factors

Management that always blames the economy, competitors, or bad luck. Great managers take responsibility and adapt. Learn from the best at KeepRule Masters.

Misaligned Incentives

Compensation tied to revenue growth or stock price rather than long-term shareholder value. People respond to incentives -- make sure they are the right ones.

Post-Investment Monitoring

The checklist does not end when you buy. Disciplined monitoring is equally important.

Quarterly Review

Compare results to your original investment thesis. Are the key assumptions still valid? Is the competitive advantage intact? Is management executing well?

Update Your Thesis

Maintain a written investment thesis for every position. When new information arrives, update it. If the thesis breaks, sell -- regardless of price action.

Know Your Exit Criteria

Define in advance what would cause you to sell: thesis violation, extreme overvaluation, or better opportunities. Never sell on emotion alone. Use KeepRule Scenarios for practice.
